# GrayMate / ป้าเหงาหนูช่วย: A Hackathon-Ready Playbook for Bringing Intergenerational Home Sharing from Japan to Thailand

## Executive Summary

Intergenerational home sharing — the deliberate matching of older adults who have a spare room with younger people (typically university students) who need affordable housing — has been a quietly proven social innovation for nearly 30 years. Programs in Spain (since 1995), France (since 2006), the Netherlands, Japan, Belgium, Canada and the United States have produced thousands of successful matches with measurable benefits: students save up to a year's tuition, seniors report dramatic reductions in loneliness, and governments offset the cost of long-term care.

For Thailand, the timing is uncannily favourable. The country is the **first developing nation to enter a fully-fledged "aged society,"** with 13.45 million people aged 60+ (20.7% of the population, June 2024) heading toward "super-aged" status by 2033. Roughly 23% of Thai elders already live alone or only with another senior, while licensed elder-care beds cover only **1.2% of the elders who would need or want them**. At the same time, university students — particularly those who migrate from upcountry to Bangkok, Chiang Mai or Khon Kaen — face accommodation costs of THB 6,000–18,000/month for a studio off-campus, often eating up half a typical family's monthly disposable income. The cultural concept of *katanyu katavedi* (กตัญญูกตเวที), reinforced by Theravada Buddhism, makes the act of caring for an unrelated elder not just acceptable but spiritually meritorious — a uniquely Thai advantage that does not exist in most Western markets.

This report shows how to weld these pieces into a winning hackathon pitch: a clear problem, an evidence-based solution, a culturally grounded business model, a working prototype concept, and impact metrics judges can quote in the deliberation room.

## 1. The Original Models from Japan

Japan, the world's most-aged society (>25% over 65, ~20% of seniors living alone), has incubated several distinct flavours of intergenerational housing — there is no single "Japanese model," but a portfolio Thai teams can borrow from.

### 1.1 Kyoto Solidaire Project (the closest analog to GrayMate)
The **Kyoto Solidaire Project** is a prefectural-government-backed home-share matching service. Four parties co-design every match: (a) the prefectural government (which provides legitimacy and elder trust), (b) accredited matching service providers, (c) university students, and (d) elderly home-providers. A peer-reviewed study published in the *Japanese Journal of Human Geography* found that students typically join *mid-degree* rather than upon arrival because trust takes time to build. Outcomes for seniors clustered into eight categories, dominated by "Opportunities for activities and exchanges" (32.1% of coded benefits) and "Psychological stability" (20.1%). Outcomes for students clustered into six categories, led by "Opportunities for new learning" (40.7%). The Kyoto study concluded that an intergenerational homeshare can become a true *home* — a supportive psychological environment — not merely cheap shelter.

### 1.2 Share Kanazawa
Share Kanazawa is a purpose-built **intergenerational community** rather than a matching service. On one site live ~40 older persons, ~32 youths with special needs, and ~8 university students, plus alpacas and an onsen (hot spring) open to neighbours. Residents can flip a name plate at the bath house so others know where they are — vital in an earthquake zone. Share Kanazawa demonstrates an "anchor" or campus model: a single physical site rather than distributed private homes.

### 1.3 The "Under One Roof Project" — NPO Matching Hongo, Tokyo
Run by NPO Matching Hongo in Bunkyo Ward (the university district of Tokyo), this project pairs seniors with spare rooms with university students who share meals several times a week. It is the cleanest small-scale "matching agency" model and the easiest to prototype.

### 1.4 Multi-generational and Senior Share Houses (Tokyo & Osaka)
Several private operators (e.g., XROSS House) run **multi-generational share houses** where students and young workers move in at below-market rent and provide light daily-living support — a structural answer to the elder-loneliness + youth-rent dilemma. A variant in Osaka pairs senior women with young foreigners for cross-cultural exchange. The format proves the unit economics can work without subsidy when the room is one in a larger shared building.

### 1.5 Yoro Shisetsu and Aoi Care (intergenerational co-located care)
Since 1976, *Yoro Shisetsu* (literally "facility for children and elderly") have combined eldercare with childcare on the same site. Aoi Care in Fujisawa weaves a public street through its buildings so schoolchildren routinely walk past elderly residents — demonstrating that **frequent, low-pressure contact** is more powerful than scheduled "intergenerational activities." A randomized study of the **REPRINTS program** in Kawasaki, published in PMC, used hierarchical linear modeling on ~891 respondents to show that long-running intergenerational programs significantly raise *neighbourhood trust* — a hard-to-buy social capital metric.

### Key Japanese policy levers worth replicating
- **Government legitimisation** (prefectural/ward-level): Kyoto's prefectural badge is what gets sceptical 80-year-olds to open their doors.
- **NPO/intermediary structure**: matching is treated as a regulated social service, not a peer-to-peer marketplace.
- **Mid-study, not orientation-day onboarding**: trust takes a semester.
- **Co-located options for those uncomfortable sharing a private home**: a critical fallback that GrayMate should plan for.

## 2. Global Examples — What Works, What Fails

### 2.1 Humanitas Deventer (Netherlands) — the viral flagship
Launched in 2012 after a 2012 Dutch funding cut to care homes, manager Gea Sijpkes invited six university students to live **rent-free** in the 160-resident nursing home in exchange for **30 hours per month** of "good neighbour" time — coffee, computer help, hosting one weekly evening meal, watching sports, celebrating birthdays. The model has been replicated in Lyon, Cleveland, the Augustinian convent in Utrecht (2004 variant), and Barcelona. Humanitas is now developing **"Woonstudent 2.0"** in which students will share apartments directly with single elders rather than just live in the same building. The model's strength is *simplicity* ("It does not require huge investments or complex care structures"); its constraint is that it requires an institutional anchor — a care home willing to convert space.

### 2.2 Ensemble2Générations (France, since 2006)
France's leading non-profit operates in 32 cities — Paris, Lyon, Marseille, Nice, Bordeaux, Lille, Toulouse, Nantes, etc. It matches students/young workers up to age 30 with seniors aged 60+ (oldest current match: 95). The senior offers a furnished room either (a) in exchange for services + token rent, or (b) in exchange for a "very modest rent" alone. Pari Solidaire is a sister Paris program. A May 2019 French study, covered by France 24, concluded that intergenerational living can simultaneously address two structural crises — affordable youth housing and elder isolation — but warned that **compatibility, not paperwork, drives outcomes**: many participants come to it as "a last resort" without realising what they are signing up for, which is the largest single failure mode.

### 2.3 Convive (Spain, since 1995) — the longest-running large-scale program
Run by NGO **Solidarios para el Desarrollo** in collaboration with Madrid's local government and seven public universities, Convive has produced **roughly 1,800 matches** since 1995. An evaluation cited in academic literature found that **75% of students' families feel safer and less worried** because their child is homesharing — a powerful family-side metric a hackathon team can borrow. Convive's success comes from (a) university partnerships that legitimise the student side, and (b) a 30-year operational track record that has produced replicated programs across **27 Spanish cities** in seven autonomous communities. Solidarios hosted the Fifth World Homeshare Congress in Madrid in 2017.

### 2.4 1toit2ages (Belgium, since 2009)
A French-speaking Belgian non-profit, structurally similar to Ensemble2Générations. Its quiet, sustained operation across more than a decade is evidence that the model is durable, not faddish.

### 2.5 Nesterly (USA — Boston & Columbus, OH)
Founded by MIT urban-planning grads Noelle Marcus and Rachel Goor and launched in **2017 as a public-private pilot with the City of Boston, the Mayor's Housing Innovation Lab, and the Elderly Commission**. It is the most directly relevant model for a hackathon team because it is a **digital platform, not a paper-based NGO**. Key Nesterly numbers worth quoting in any pitch:

- **80+ applications in the first three weeks** of the pilot.
- **8 successful matches** in the pilot (small but the program later scaled to a target of 100 matches by June 2020).
- **Trulia research cited by the program: 3.6 million spare bedrooms** in U.S. baby-boomer homes in metro areas; **38,000+ in Boston alone**.
- Graduate students could **save up to USD 24,000 per year** versus a one-bedroom apartment.
- Average rent in matches: **~USD 750/month**.
- Revenue model: a modest contract fee, **2.5% commission on rent from each side**.
- Recognition: MIT IDEAS Global Challenge "Idea to Change the World" (2017); coverage in The Atlantic, Forbes, NPR's Marketplace.

Nesterly's stack — background checks, digital rental agreements, payment rails, structured exchange of chores in lieu of rent (snow shovelling, cooking, taking out the trash) — is the closest off-the-shelf reference architecture for GrayMate.

### 2.6 Other Worth-Knowing Programs
- **Canada HomeShare / SpacesShared** (Toronto, with university partners): claims that a participating student saves **an entire year's tuition in eight months** versus market rent.
- **HomeShare Vermont** and **New York Foundation for Senior Citizens** (the only NYC service of its kind, free of charge).
- **BytSpolu** in Pilsen, Czech Republic (mentored by ensemble2générations) — proof the model travels to mid-sized university cities.
- **Switzerland**: programs developed independently to address student housing pressure.

### 2.7 What Makes Programs Succeed or Fail — the Evidence
A scoping review in Oxford Academic's *Innovation in Aging* (Bodkin & Saxena and others) and Jaffe & Wellin's foundational 1989 study converge on a single conclusion: **success or failure is determined by whether the home-provider's expectations for support and companionship are met by the home-seeker.** The implication for a hackathon prototype is that **expectation-management tooling (structured intake, written compatibility profiles, mid-tenancy check-ins, mediator hotline)** is more valuable than the matching algorithm. Other recurring failure modes:
- Treating the program as a last-resort housing fix rather than a relationship.
- Lack of cultural-difference navigation (e.g., elder unfamiliarity with student mental-health needs or LGBTQ+ identity).
- Insufficient ongoing support after the match is made.
- Family-side anxiety on both sides — the senior's children worrying about a stranger; the student's parents worrying about caregiving expectations.

## 3. Adapting the Model to Thailand

### 3.1 Thailand's Aging-Society Statistics — the Demand Side

A WHO-cited estimate finds **a quarter of older adults globally experience social isolation and loneliness**, and Thailand sits squarely in that bracket. A 2023 Pengpid & Peltzer study in *BMC Psychology* documented persistent loneliness among middle-aged and older Thais; multiple local studies (Aroonsrimorakot et al., 2022; Phuangcharoen & Thayansin, 2022) link loneliness to depression, alcohol misuse, and reduced ADL performance — all of which carry direct healthcare costs the Thai state increasingly cannot absorb. The World Bank and Holt-Lunstad's landmark meta-analysis (2015) classify chronic loneliness as a mortality risk on par with smoking 15 cigarettes a day.

### 3.2 Thai Student Housing — the Supply Side of the Match

Thailand has roughly 2 million tertiary students, with the largest concentrations in Bangkok and a heavy migration flow from Isaan (the north-east) and the North to Bangkok-Pathum Thani-Nonthaburi (Chulalongkorn, Mahidol, Thammasat, Kasetsart) and to Chiang Mai University and Khon Kaen University. Indicative monthly costs (THB):

| Housing type | Bangkok | Chiang Mai | Khon Kaen |
|---|---|---|---|
| University dorm (shared) | 2,500–7,000 | 2,000–6,500 | 1,500–5,000 |
| Off-campus studio | 6,000–18,000 | 3,500–10,000 | 6,000–9,000 |
| Modern condo / serviced | 12,000–20,000+ | 7,000+ | 6,000–9,000 |

By contrast, a typical upcountry household income is well under THB 20,000/month — meaning a Bangkok studio can **consume 30–90% of family income**. University dorms (such as Mahidol's Salaya halls at THB 3,000–8,000/semester) are heavily oversubscribed and closed during the June–July break. The pain is sharpest for first-generation university students from Isaan — exactly the demographic that produces the highest rate of dropouts from financial pressure.

The arithmetic: an empty room in an empty-nest Bangkok household, rented to a student at **THB 3,000–5,000/month including utilities** (versus a market studio at THB 12,000), saves the student family **THB 84,000–108,000/year** while putting **THB 36,000–60,000** into the senior's pocket — a non-trivial top-up to Thailand's average old-age allowance, which is far below a living wage.

### 3.3 Cultural Fit — Why Thailand May Be Better Suited Than Europe

Three Thai cultural assets give GrayMate an advantage Western programs lack:

**กตัญญูกตเวที (*katanyu katavedi*) — gratitude and the obligation to repay.** A foundational Thai-Buddhist value with three dimensions, articulated in the work of Chulalongkorn nursing scholar Jiraporn Kespichayawattana and others: *bun khun* (the total benefits parents bestow), *katanyu* (the felt sense of gratitude), and *katavedi* (the obligation to repay through action). Crucially, *katanyu* in Thai culture extends **beyond biological parents to "elders we respect and are grateful to"** — opening the door to non-kin care relationships. Buddhism teaches that returning favours is meritorious and that filial piety is one of the highest virtues; a 2022 Mahidol/Walailak ethnography of "Ya-Luk-Ka-Tan-Yoo" (filial-piety medicine) documents how Thai families translate this value into concrete acts of care even when geography separates generations. For a student, sharing a home with an unrelated *yai* or *pu* (grandmother/grandfather figure) is not transactional charity — it is **culturally intelligible and socially admired**.

**The decline of the multi-generational household.** EPIC's ethnographic research on Thai cultural frictions documents that traditional multi-generational living is eroding under urbanisation, smaller families, and youth migration — even as Thai society still expects filial care. This produces a generation of empty-nest seniors who *want* a household to feel "alive and full" again, and a generation of urban-migrant students who *miss* the multi-generational warmth they grew up with. GrayMate is not asking Thais to adopt a foreign concept; it is offering a market-based way to restore something Thai culture already validates.

**Buddhist temple infrastructure as a trust anchor.** Unlike France or the Netherlands, Thailand has a network of ~40,000 Buddhist temples (*wat*) that already function as community centres and elder gathering points. Temples can serve as the GrayMate "trust layer" — places where seniors and student candidates first meet, and where monks and lay leaders can mediate disputes. This is a culturally native equivalent of the prefectural-government legitimacy that makes Kyoto Solidaire work.

### 3.4 Existing Thai Programs and Policy Context

Thailand already has fragments of the puzzle, none of which currently combine into a home-share model:

- **2,400 elderly schools and 2,064 elderly development centres** nationwide, run with support from the Ministry of Social Development and Human Security.
- **55,000+ trained elder-care officials** and a 2024 pilot training 40 community caregivers in 12 provinces.
- The **Department of Health's Baan RuenRom Elderly Day Care Centre** (Ministry of Public Health), aligned with WHO's ICOPE framework — a strong potential pilot partner.
- The **20-Year National Strategy (2017–2036)** and the 12th National Economic and Social Development Plan, both of which explicitly call for innovation to address the aging society.
- The **Department of Older Persons** under the MSDHS supports entrepreneurship by elders (130,000+ recipients of business support).
- The **Village Health Volunteer (อสม.) network** — already embedded in every sub-district — could screen elder applicants.
- **Asian Centre for Active Ageing (ACAI)**, hosted in Thailand since 2020, is an explicit ASEAN hub for ageing innovation.
- **University-side**: Chulalongkorn, Mahidol, Thammasat, Kasetsart, Chiang Mai University and Khon Kaen University all run student-services offices that maintain housing lists — natural distribution partners.

**Critically: there is no existing intergenerational home-share matching service in Thailand.** The whitespace is real.

### 3.5 Regulatory and Legal Considerations

The single most important legal fact for GrayMate is the **Hotel Act B.E. 2547 (2004) and its B.E. 2551 (2008) amendments**:

- Renting accommodation for **less than 30 consecutive days** is regulated as a hotel and requires a hotel licence, with fines up to **THB 200,000 administrative / THB 500,000 criminal and possible imprisonment** for unlicensed operation.
- Renting for **30 days or more is treated as a normal residential lease and is exempt** — this is the legal lane GrayMate must operate in. Student tenancies of one semester or one academic year fit this lane perfectly.
- A small-operator exemption exists under the 2008 Ministerial Regulation: properties with **≤4 rooms and ≤20 guests**, run as supplementary income, may operate with simple registration at the local district office.
- **Condominium juristic persons** can impose stricter rules than national law allows; many Bangkok condos prohibit subletting outright. GrayMate should focus initial recruitment on **detached houses (บ้านเดี่ยว) and townhouses owned by Thai senior citizens** — exactly the housing stock baby-boomer Bangkokians live in.
- **TM30 reporting**: hosts must report any non-Thai resident to immigration within 24 hours. For Thai-only matches this is moot, but for international students it adds compliance overhead.
- **Income tax**: rental income is taxable. The Boston/Nesterly playbook of issuing a structured rental agreement at signing is directly applicable.
- **Consumer Protection Act, B.E. 2522 (1979)** governs lease agreements — GrayMate's standard contract template must comply.
- **Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA, 2019)**: any matching platform that stores ID-card scans, health information, or family-contact data must register a data controller and implement consent flows.

The cleanest legal positioning: GrayMate is a **non-profit social-enterprise matching service**, not a rental platform; tenancies are 30+ days; the platform takes a small service fee, not commission on rent; and a written semester-or-longer lease is signed offline in compliance with Thai contract law.

## 4. The Pain Points — Hard Numbers for the Pitch

A judging panel will remember three or four numbers. Here is the curated list a Thai team should commit to memory, drawn from the strongest sources available:

**On the elder side:**
- **13.45 million** Thais aged 60+ — 20.7% of the population (DPA, June 2024).
- **23.2%** of Thai elders live alone; another 12% live only with another senior (NESDC, 2019; Situation of Thai Older Persons).
- The proportion of older adults living alone has **roughly tripled in 20 years** (3.6% in 1994 → ~12% of households today; NSO).
- Elders living alone are ~2× more likely than co-resident elders to live below the national poverty line (38.75% vs 19.39%; Meemon & Chun Paek, *Asia-Pacific Social Science Review*, 2020).
- Existing elder-housing supply covers **just 1.2%** of elders living alone who might need it (Real Estate Information Center, 2023).
- **5.3 million** Thai elders have low or no income (Department of Older Persons).
- **628,800** Thai elders have dementia (NESDC, 2019).
- Loneliness mortality risk is comparable to smoking 15 cigarettes/day (Holt-Lunstad et al., 2015).
- Thailand will become a **super-aged society by 2033** — only ~7 years away.

**On the student side:**
- Off-campus Bangkok studios cost **THB 6,000–18,000/month**, easily 3–6× a dorm bed.
- Total monthly student living cost (Bangkok, ex-rent): **THB 12,000–16,000** — meaning total cost of attendance can hit THB 25,000–35,000/month against an upcountry median household income often below THB 20,000.
- University dorms close for the **June–July break**, displacing students twice a year.
- Bangkok household size has shrunk from **5.7 (1970) to 3.2 (2010)** — meaning a vast latent supply of empty rooms. Although Thailand-specific data is not yet aggregated, a Trulia-equivalent inventory exercise would likely identify **hundreds of thousands of spare bedrooms** in elder-headed households in the Bangkok Metropolitan Region alone (the U.S. analog: 38,000 in Boston; 3.6 million nationwide).

**The killer composite metric for the slide:**
> *"23% of Thai elders live alone in homes with empty rooms. 1.2% of them can access institutional care. Meanwhile, 1 in 3 university students from upcountry spends over half their family's income on a single rented room. GrayMate can solve both — at zero infrastructure cost."*

## 5. What Wins a Social-Impact Hackathon — Pitch Architecture

Synthesised from TAIKAI, FasterCapital, AngelHack, David Beckett's pitch-coaching framework, Circles.Life's internal hackathon retrospective, and Jorge Ferreiro's interview with McKinsey's Jake Hart:

### 5.1 The 3-minute structure judges expect (≈400 words, 27 sentences)
1. **Hook (0:00–0:15)** — One human story, not a statistic. *"Grandma Somying, 78, in Phra Khanong, has three empty rooms and hasn't spoken to anyone in three days. Twenty kilometres away, Tan, an engineering student from Khon Kaen, just got an electricity bill that means he'll skip lunch this week."*
2. **Problem (0:15–0:45)** — The composite metric above; one elder stat, one student stat, one cultural stat (*katanyu*).
3. **Solution + USP (0:45–1:30)** — GrayMate / ป้าเหงาหนูช่วย: a temple- and university-anchored matching platform that pairs verified seniors with verified students for 30+ day leases at 30–50% of market rent in exchange for companionship and light help. Frame the USP in three words: *Trusted. Cultural. Legal.*
4. **Demo (1:30–2:15)** — Live walk-through of the prototype: the Thai-language LINE bot or mobile app intake → compatibility quiz → temple/university-verified profile → match recommendation → e-contract → payment escrow. Judges remember demos, not slides.
5. **Impact + Business Model (2:15–2:45)** — Quantified social impact + revenue path (see §5.3). Use the Boston/Nesterly proof point: 80 applications in 3 weeks; 1,800 Convive matches in Madrid since 1995.
6. **Ask + Future (2:45–3:00)** — The one thing you need from the judges: pilot partners (a wat, a university student-services office, the Department of Older Persons) and seed funding for 100 pilot matches in 12 months.

### 5.2 Judging criteria the team should optimise against
TAIKAI's six published criteria — **creativity & innovation, technical execution, functional MVP, problem-solving relevance, impact & potential, final pitch quality** — map almost perfectly to social-impact rubrics. Specifically, judges rate higher when teams:
- **Show a working prototype**, not slideware. Build at minimum a clickable Figma flow plus a working LINE-bot intake.
- **Tell a story**, not a spec sheet. A single named *yai* and a single named student outperform any chart.
- **Quantify the social return on investment (SROI)** — every THB 1 of platform cost saves THB X in care-home subsidy, THB Y in student housing aid, and THB Z in mental-health treatment.
- **Demonstrate scalability with constraints**: the team has thought through PDPA, the Hotel Act, condo bylaws, and family-side anxieties.
- **Keep to time**. Beckett's data: overrunning a 3-minute pitch is the most common reason teams lose.

### 5.3 Framing as a hybrid social + business opportunity
Hackathon judges, especially those drawn from venture/CSR backgrounds, want to see *both* a defensible social mission and a credible revenue model. The Nesterly hybrid is the template:

| Revenue stream | Who pays | Inspired by |
|---|---|---|
| Match success fee | Student family (one-time, THB 500–1,500) | Nesterly contract fee |
| Platform commission | 2.5% of monthly rent from each side | Nesterly |
| Premium "verified host" badge | Senior household (free for the first 1,000) | Marketplaces |
| University B2B contract | University student-services offices pay an annual licence to access matched housing for their upcountry intake | Convive (Spain, 7 universities) |
| Insurance bundle | Optional liability insurance via a Thai insurer; affiliate margin | Nesterly partnerships |
| Government / SDGs grants | TGRI, Department of Older Persons, MSDHS, ACAI, WHO Thailand | Boston Mayor's Housing Innovation Lab |
| CSR sponsorships | Banks (Krungsri silver-economy reports), property developers, eldercare firms | Nesterly's MIT IDEAS funding |

**Unit-economics sketch for the pitch slide:**
- 1,000 matches in Year 2 × THB 4,000 avg monthly rent × 2.5% × 2 sides = **THB 200,000/month revenue (≈ THB 2.4M/year)** from commission alone, plus match fees, university contracts, and CSR grants.
- Compared to estimated SROI: 1,000 elders moved from "isolated" to "companioned" — using WHO-cited per-capita mental-health-care offset estimates — saves the Thai healthcare system multiples of that figure.

### 5.4 Pitfalls to avoid (from the pitch-coaching literature and from real homeshare programs)
- **Don't over-promise care.** Make it crystal-clear: students are *good neighbours*, **not caregivers** (Sijpkes' line at Humanitas). This protects both sides legally and emotionally.
- **Don't bury the cultural fit in a footnote.** Lead with *katanyu* — it is the single biggest reason GrayMate Thailand can succeed where Western programs hit cultural friction.
- **Don't use a Western gender-blind matching algorithm uncritically.** Bodkin & Saxena's scoping review found that mismatched expectations around gender, sexuality, and mental health are the #1 driver of failed matches. Build a Thai-context compatibility quiz.
- **Don't claim Thai families will be on board automatically.** Convive's 75% "family safer and less worried" metric was earned by long onboarding; budget for elder-children family meetings.
- **Don't ignore safety theatre.** Background checks, Thai national ID verification, university-issued letters of standing, temple/abbot referrals — all must be visible in the demo even if backend is faked.

## 7. Risks, Honest Caveats, and What the Evidence Does *Not* Yet Show

A winning pitch acknowledges what it does not know. Three honest caveats:

1. **No published Thai pilot exists yet** to confirm uptake rates. Boston's 80 applications in 3 weeks and Madrid's 1,800 matches over 30 years are encouraging proxies, but cultural translation always carries risk. The pitch should propose a 100-match, 12-month pilot precisely to generate Thai-context evidence.
2. **The 23.2% "elders living alone" figure (NESDC 2019) and the 12% household figure (Situation of Thai Older Persons 2020) are not identical**; they measure different things (individual vs household) and a hackathon team should be careful which it cites where. The Department of Provincial Administration's 13,450,391 figure for June 2024 is the safest demographic anchor.
3. **Several "demand-side" student housing figures come from study-abroad and commercial sites** (ThaiRanked, Mastersportal, Asia Exchange) rather than peer-reviewed sources. They are directionally accurate but a pitch citing them should describe them as "indicative market rates" rather than definitive statistics. The Government Housing Bank's REIC and the Department of Older Persons are the gold-standard Thai sources.
4. **Replication is not automatic.** Bodkin & Saxena's 2017 review found that mismatched expectations sink matches across every country studied; the Thai program will only work if the matching, mediation, and exit infrastructure is *better* than the headline matchmaking algorithm.

## 8. Closing Argument

The ingredients for a winning Thai intergenerational home-share platform are unusually well-aligned: an enormous and rapidly growing senior population with empty rooms and shrinking incomes; a captive student market with severe and worsening housing pressure; a deep cultural script (*katanyu katavedi*) that *already* sanctifies care for non-kin elders; an institutional trust infrastructure (temples, universities, the Village Health Volunteer network) that no Western program enjoys; a clean legal lane (Hotel Act 30+ day exemption); 30 years of validated operating models from Madrid, Paris, Deventer, Kyoto and Boston to copy; and a national policy environment (20-Year Strategy, ACAI, Department of Older Persons) actively soliciting innovation. A team that walks into a hackathon with the demographic numbers memorised, a working LINE-bot prototype, a temple-and-university anchor partnership, and a clear THB unit-economics slide will be very hard to beat.

ป้าเหงาหนูช่วย — *Aunty's lonely, I'll help.* The phrase is the pitch.
